01 · The question

Where is the corridor, and what's growing in it?

Two classification problems, one pixel at a time, at 10-metre Sentinel-2 resolution:

Extent — riparian vs other

Is this pixel riparian vegetation, or the surrounding water / agriculture / bare ground? A solved problem — so it's our control: does the pipeline even work?

Invasives — invasive vs native

Inside the corridor: tamarisk / Russian-olive, or cottonwood / willow? This is the actual product — and the hard one.

The discriminator is phenology — the seasonal rhythm of leaf-out and senescence. Tamarisk greens and browns on a different schedule, with a distinct short-wave-infrared water signature. Critically, plain NDVI is near-random for this (AUC ≈ 0.5); the signal lives in SWIR/red-edge, so the models get the full spectral time series, not a greenness index.
02 · The data cube

A bounding box becomes a phenology cube

For any reach we stream Sentinel-2 straight from a public catalog and composite it into 12 monthly median mosaics — the seasonal trajectory, built identically for every reach so they're comparable. Only the corridor windows are kept; whole scenes are never downloaded.

A finding that cost us a number. We first tried a cheap shortcut — one satellite scene per month instead of the aligned median mosaics. A transfer test collapsed to AUC 0.37 — worse than random. Not a real failure: the single scenes sampled different phenological moments, so the features were out-of-distribution. Rebuilt on the identical compositing, the same test scored 0.80. Aligned compositing isn't a detail — it is the experiment.
03 · The two models

A Random Forest vs a fine-tuned foundation model

RF Random Forest

~300 decision trees voting on each pixel's 144 spectral-temporal numbers. Per-pixel, no spatial context, cheap, no GPU. Bagging makes it hard to overfit — a genuinely strong baseline.

FM OlmoEarth, fine-tuned

A ~207-million-parameter vision transformer pretrained on vast satellite imagery, then fine-tuned on our labels. Sees spatial context; predicts a per-pixel map. The bet: pretraining transfers where a from-scratch model can't.

The whole question is fine-tuning. Frozen foundation-model features lose to RF; whether fine-tuning wins is what a GPU buys — so we measured it, honestly.

We corrected two of our own claims here: the “0.90–0.92” baseline was measured a different (easier) way, and the reference labels are noisy — so the 0.06 RF-over-FM gap sits within the label-noise envelope. On the easy control, RF is at least as good, for free.

Across a real 50 km + 47%→82% prevalence gap, RF and the fine-tuned foundation model tie exactly on AUC (0.80) — their ranking ability transferred equally. The FM's low F1 at the default threshold (0.56 vs RF's 0.68) is threshold miscalibration under the prevalence shift, not a representation failure — recalibrated, it's 0.84. So the GPU buys no transfer advantage (the whole premise of the bet), but the model doesn't fail either.

Why this is the verdict. In-domain scores always flatter. Cross-reach transfer is the only setting where overfitting has nowhere to hide — and here it's a tie. RF stays the pragmatic choice (same ranking, cheaper, better-calibrated out of the box); "RF beats FM" would overstate it.

The honest positioning

  • Riparian extent is already solved for this basin (CO-RIP, κ 0.80) — RF reproduces it; that's the control, not the contribution.
  • Tamarisk detection is established; the discriminator is phenology / SWIR, not NDVI.
  • The tamarisk beetle inverts the greenness signal in this basin — a decline can be biocontrol working, not recovery.
  • What doesn't exist yet: an annual, 10 m, beetle-aware, native-vs-invasive product. That's the target.
